# Giovanni di Venezia

## Hook

A priest-assassin who only kills when he can call it justice — and who is learning, on the road east, how thin that word has worn.

## Identity

Father Giovanni di Venezia: born a farmer's third son in the hill country north of Venice, apprenticed to a merchant, ordained at twenty-five, and broken open well before his thirtieth year. Olive-skinned, lean, calloused at the hands long after the plow; a white scar at the ribs from a Patriarch's bodyguard in Aquileia. He carries a worn merchant's dagger, a crucifix at his throat, and a quarterstaff. What people notice first is the stillness — the posture of a man who has already decided where the exits are, dressed in the robes of one whose vocation should have made such calculations unnecessary. Then they notice the voice, trained equally for haggling, homiletics, and the moment before a kill.

## Origin in brief

Sent as the junior member of a curial inquiry to the Monastery of Saint Severus in the Apennines, Giovanni watched Brother Aldric's heretical liturgy thin a stone door into something that was not stone, and watched what came through it take Father Matthias's face and Father Clement's mind. He carried the scholar Clement out alive and dying both, buried what remained of Matthias under the henbane in the monastery garden, and came down out of the mountains a smaller and colder believer than he had gone up. Years later, in Venice, Bishop Paulus put that wound to use — turned the country boy who had once told a merchant he was cheating his father into the Church's quiet blade, until a frame-up at the Patriarch of Aquileia opened him along the ribs and sent him fleeing east, with a knife always one bathhouse behind him.

## Role in the party

Giovanni is the company's confessor, its inspirer in battle, and — when the work demands it — its quietest killer. He has the merchant's eye for a lie and the assassin's eye for a throat, and he uses both. With **Bansi** he begins as the priest who catches her stealing from Hind's coffers and refuses to report her; the friction shades, over months, into a working trust between two people who recognize each other's instincts. With **Eskil** he is brother-keeper, the voice that hauls the Norseman back from Akivasha's pool, the hand that cracks a quarterstaff across his chest when prayer alone will not break a vision. With **Lüèduó** he is the Catholic priest learning to ride beside something serpent-blooded and other, and choosing — episode by episode — to keep riding. None of the four trust easily. Giovanni is often the one who insists they try.

## Arc through the journey

He arrives in Samarkand a hunted man and is nearly killed in a textile market before he has finished thinking about a bath. He prays the dead off a castle wall in the village past the Iron Gates; he dies a little when ghouls answer him with whispers instead of silence. In Merv his crucifix steadies an Earmack who has ridden out of horror; in the catacombs he gives her last rites with her hand in his, and learns that his Latin is good for something even here. In Shiraz a vampire wearing a freed slave's face opens his throat in a stranger's library, and the healer stitches him without opium because Giovanni asks for the hurt as penance. Beneath the Egyptian sands at Ikhmindi he reaches for the Book of Eibon and a mummy's hand reaches back; he watches Isaac's mind drawn out through a hollow talon and loses, in that chamber, the last clean version of what he thought his Church understood about evil. In Constantinople he kneels alone in a Western-rite church and tries to make the reconciliation come, and it does not. In Itil he meets another of the Church's blades praying at a frontier shrine, and answers him as one killer answers another — neither lie, nor truth. In the Cathedral of Bones his preaching makes Karishkir flinch on her throne of pelvises; when Drekhlo's runesword pins Eskil through the spine and Giovanni grips the hilt to free him, the blade shows him a green-lit room and a version of himself who kills for the pleasure of watching the light go out. He recognizes the man.

## Thematic note

Giovanni is the rationalization of violence under the cover of righteousness — and the slow, unwilled erosion of that cover by what the Silk Road is teaching him about the shape of the cosmos. Marco the merchant warned him the Church would polish the boy out of him by calling cleverness wisdom and ruthlessness zeal; Paulus made the prophecy true. Every kill east of Venice has needed less doctrine and more honesty to justify, and the doctrine has begun to feel inadequate to the things it is supposed to name. The cursed runesword that passes to him at the end of Episode VII is not a weapon. It is penance — a leash he asks to wear because no one else can be trusted with it, and because the blade has already shown him what waits if he ever stops saying no. He carries it now against his spine, listening, refusing.
