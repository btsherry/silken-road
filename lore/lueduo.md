# Lüèduó

## Hook
A Uyghur dancer-spy whose every grace is borrowed, and the lender keeps a ledger.

## Identity
**Lüèduó**, called Raven — a name granted by an Emperor for the black of his hair and the way he moved beneath it. Born Quzghun among the horse-clans at the edge of the Taklamakan, taken east as a Tang hostage, made over into a creature of the Dragon Throne. High cheekbones, a mouth that "curved cruel and kind by turns," hair black as a wet bough falling straight to his shoulders. His eyes are the tell: golden-amber, and in certain lights the pupils narrow to vertical slits and refuse to round again. Beneath sleeves and collar, a faint iridescence rides his skin where the scales are learning his shape.

## Origin in brief
Something old was already curled in his blood when he was born — a diluted line of the Nagini's ancient servants, dormant for generations until the shamans of his clan saw the door behind his eyes and refused to teach him to open it. Sent east as a hostage, he danced his way from war-prize to imperial favorite to spy in the bed-chambers of the court, harvesting secrets for the eunuch spymaster Wei. An affair with Lady Mei, the wife of a councillor too powerful to cuckold, ended with her dragged to her death and Lüèduó banished west on a "die usefully if you can" assignment that delivered him to a buried temple called the Serpent's Rest. The Nagini was waiting. She had always been waiting. She bit him, claimed him, and opened the debt that he has been paying ever since.

## Role in the party
The party's mouth and its eavesdropper. Lüèduó speaks every tongue the Silk Road sells — Turkic, Sogdian, the trade Arabic, court Chinese, the dialects he picks up between caravans — and he reads a room's loyalties in thirty seconds, a habit Wei drilled into him before the curse made it instinct. He is the one who finds Eskil at the smithy in Penjikent and recognizes a fellow cursed soul; the one who first marks Nizack as false near the Iron Gates and quietly tells Bansi to watch him; the one who reaches Giovanni's bleeding throat in Shiraz and refuses to let him die in the street. With Bansi he runs a thieves' shorthand of glances and unspoken contracts. With Eskil he keeps a wary brotherhood of two damned men who do not need to discuss the damnation. With Giovanni he discovers, slowly and with reluctance, that he is capable of loyalty that is not also leverage.

## Arc through the journey
He begins the journey as a calculator in dancer's silks — a man who reflexively files every encounter as source, asset, or threat. The cursed castle past the Iron Gates is the first invoice: cornered at the altar with the bird-thing closing, he begs the Nagini for help, and she gives it, and the puncture-marks at his wrist itch every time he has been tempted since. In Merv, summoned in a vision in the middle of a seduction, he is reminded that he tells "pretty taless" but lies; the Nagini's tone has gone proprietary. In Shiraz he gambles, debauches with Sadia, steals a self-playing lute from a room of frozen corpses — the old reaching, undimmed. Aidhab brings the first imperial agent he has seen in years, Fashian, with a polite extortion: deliver the amulet or be hunted. He notes the offer and does not take it, and is quietly surprised at himself. Beneath the temple at Ikhmindi he uses the Nagini's gift on Akivasha — catches her spell like a mirror catches light and reverses it — and buys the company the seconds they need to run. In Constantinople he learns from the Nagini that he needs a holding-charm. On the Volga he discovers that a bounty has followed him to the world's edge: someone wants him brought east, alive, for a sum that would let any thief retire. In the bone cathedral he draws the lycanthropy out of Sadia and into himself, lets the Nagini constrict it, and pays in blood from his nose and a flicker of light gone from his eyes. By the time the company rides south from the burning fortress, the boy who reached for everything and held nothing has begun, against his nature, to hold these particular six people. The ledger does not care. The ledger is patient.

## Thematic note
He is the novel's accountant of debt. Every supernatural transaction in his life carries a running balance — the Nagini's gift in the cursed castle, the visions in Merv, the spell turned back on Akivasha, the curse pulled out of Sadia. She tallies. He can feel her tallying. He is the most fluent of the four protagonists — in languages, in rooms, in the architecture of magical control "from the inside" — and he is the most owned. The freedom he performs is rented; the grace is borrowed; the very pupils of his eyes belong to someone else. Where Eskil bears his curse as a weight to be resisted and Giovanni bears his sins as a penance to be carried, Lüèduó bears his as a debt to be serviced, indefinitely, with interest. He reaches and reaches — for fire, for forbidden dances, for Lady Mei, for the dice, for the lute, for the chance to save a friend — and every reach is added to the column. The compulsion that emptied his purse at the caravanserai is the same compulsion that opens the door for the Nagini each time she knocks. He knows it. He plays anyway.
