# Bansi

## Hook

A princess who picked her own locks, a thief who picked Kali's, and the youngest blade in a company that mostly forgets she is the most dangerous person in the room.

## Identity

**Name:** Bansi (born Iksha Bhima, thirteenth child and fifth daughter of the Raja of Sonapura). The name she chose for herself means *flute* in the old tongue.
**Origin:** Kashmir, by way of Multan and Hormuz and a coastal shrine at Quilon.
**Appearance:** Small, fast, deceptively unobtrusive — the build of someone who moves through crowds without disturbing them. Her hands are her most legible feature: princess's bones under hundreds of fresh, fine scars left when Kali "cut out what did not belong," so profuse the skin has changed color. Between her brows, a cold spot the tantrika's thumb pressed into her — Kali's mark, invisible and always present. A sickle slung across her back as a "tool" rather than a weapon, a brace of fine balanced daggers beneath, a wooden flute her mother gave her, and a length of razor-wire disguised as jewelry. A short braid of rope tied at her wrist, salvaged from the unfinished splice of a sailor named Tarek.

## Origin in brief

Iksha was a chess piece her family forgot to play until the price was right — promised at thirteen as sixth wife to a fifty-three-year-old Shahi. Overhearing her own bride-price quoted in her uncle's voice, she walked out of the palace dressed as a servant boy, took her name from a flute, and fell in with a westbound caravan. In Multan the Bahn-Sa-Sahn — a thieves' guild — taught her locks under Dalir, who moved like a dancer, and broke her on the mat under Arjun until she learned that survival was a craft, not a gift. Two years later, after a job in Hormuz that ended a slaver ring and put her on a Yemeni dhow, the storm took her friend Tarek and showed her what waited under the water. At Quilon she was drawn to a shrine she had not been seeking, and an old tantrika put her on the floor of Kali's awareness and said: *you belong to time, and time belongs to her.*

## Role in the party

Bansi is the company's eyes, hands, and lockpick — the one sent up the wall, into the upper embrasure, alone to the wharf-side guild, alone to the pig-farmer informant. She runs point with Giovanni in every dungeon from Ikhmindi to the Night Wolves' keep; she trades information with Lüèduó in low voices over a whetstone and tells no one else; she covers Eskil's flank when his curse pulls him toward something he cannot resist, and slaps him hard enough across the helm to bring him back. She is the youngest of the four — barely past the palace, in some weathers — and the most professionally trained in violence. The "Daughter of Dancers" inheritance from Dalir is visible whenever she fights: economy, angle, the curved blade where a sword would announce itself. She steals casually to settle group bills, picks pockets through Merv to pay for Sadia's wine, and slips Gan a pouch of road-money on parting. She trusts slowly and protects fiercely; the line *He is under my protection. I will defend him with my life* is what she says about Lüèduó when a slaver-guild handler asks after his price.

## Arc through the journey

In Samarkand she joins the company sideways, kicking a tent-pole into an assassin and dropping rather more weapons than seemed possible at the guards' feet. By Merv she is sitting closer to the fire than she did in Episode I but not yet *among* — old habits — and she plays counterpoint flute to Rahzalahn's oud, the winds of Kashmir under the melody. In Shiraz she invests her share in steel: a singing scimitar and two weeks' brutal drilling with a Persian master, choosing to grow as a fighter rather than rest. Under the Egyptian sands she pulls Eskil back from the Feaster's pool by force and saves the company on the retreat through her own disabled trap — but counts heads after and has to say the words about Isaac. In Constantinople she walks into a hostile thieves' guild alone and changes her face on the spot. In the Night Wolves' keep she goes over the palisade first, throws the poisoned daggers that pin Revna's arms, and somersaults from the gallery to take the huntress's head clean off mid-transformation. Karishkir's mind-sorcery breaks against her: *the queen's sorcery found no purchase on a soul that had been armored by disappointment and promised to Kali.* She ends the book having grown into command, and refusing the cursed sword on principle — she has seen what one collar costs and will not take another.

## Thematic note

Bansi is what survival looks like when you decline to become anyone's instrument. Every authority that has tried to own her — palace, guild, slaver, queen, even the goddess — has gotten skill back instead of obedience. Her great motif is the lock: every lock is a language, and she would rather understand a thing than break it. Kali's claiming is the one debt she did not pick herself and cannot pick open; *kala* is time, and time always comes due. She carries the mark and the warmth and the void between heartbeats where her flute now finds notes that belong to no scale she knows. She is the youngest, the smallest, the most scarred, and — of the four — the one most likely to come through intact, because she long ago accepted that nothing in this world is given and the only honest thing she has ever felt is the stillness underneath all of it.
